Signs of Ovulation: What Your Body Is Telling You

Published July 1, 2026  ·  5 min read

Your body sends a series of subtle — and sometimes not-so-subtle — signals each month to let you know that ovulation is approaching or has just occurred. Learning to recognize these signs can be genuinely empowering, whether you're hoping to conceive or simply want a deeper understanding of your own cycle.

1. Changes in Cervical Mucus

One of the most reliable signs of ovulation is a change in cervical mucus — the fluid your cervix produces throughout your cycle. In the days leading up to ovulation, discharge becomes clearer, more slippery, and stretchy, often described as resembling raw egg whites. This consistency makes it easier for sperm to travel through the reproductive tract. If your cervical mucus has that egg-white quality, your fertile window is likely open.

2. A Rise in Basal Body Temperature

Basal body temperature (BBT) is your body's temperature at complete rest. Just after ovulation, a slight but consistent rise — typically 0.2°C to 0.5°C (about 0.4°F to 1°F) — occurs due to rising progesterone levels. Tracking your BBT daily with a sensitive thermometer, taken first thing in the morning before you get out of bed, can help you identify this pattern over time. While the temperature shift confirms ovulation has already happened, patterns across several cycles can help you predict future fertile windows.

3. Mittelschmerz — Mid-Cycle Pain

Some people experience a mild, one-sided pelvic ache or twinge around ovulation time. This is known as mittelschmerz, German for "middle pain." It can feel like a dull cramp, a sharp jab, or a bloated sensation on one side of the lower abdomen, and it may last anywhere from a few minutes to a few hours. It's not harmful, but it's a helpful real-time signal that an egg may be releasing.

4. Increased Sex Drive

Nature has a way of nudging things along. Many people notice a natural increase in libido around ovulation — a result of rising estrogen and luteinizing hormone (LH). If you find yourself feeling more interested in intimacy mid-cycle, your body may be doing exactly what it's designed to do.

5. Breast Tenderness or Heightened Senses

Hormonal shifts around ovulation can also bring mild breast sensitivity, nipple tenderness, or a heightened sense of smell. Some research suggests that smell sensitivity peaks around ovulation — an interesting quirk of reproductive biology. If you find everyday scents suddenly more noticeable mid-cycle, it may be worth noting the timing.

6. LH Surge on an Ovulation Test

Over-the-counter ovulation predictor kits (OPKs) detect a surge in luteinizing hormone, which typically occurs 24 to 36 hours before ovulation. A positive test — usually indicated by a line as dark or darker than the control line — means ovulation is imminent. For those actively trying to conceive, OPKs can be one of the most direct ways to time intercourse.

Putting It All Together

No single sign is definitive on its own. The most accurate picture comes from combining multiple observations: tracking your cycle length, monitoring cervical mucus, charting your BBT, and using OPKs when needed. Over a few months, patterns will emerge that are specific to you and your body.
